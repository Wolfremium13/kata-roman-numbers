Kata Roman Numbers is a programming exercise that involves converting integers to Roman numeral equivalents, and vice versa. In this kata, the challenge is to create a program that can convert Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3, etc.) to Roman numerals (I, II, III, etc.) and vice versa.

The Roman numeral system uses a combination of letters from the Latin alphabet to represent numbers. The symbols used are I, V, X, L, C, D, and M, which respectively represent the numbers 1, 5, 10, 50, 100, 500, and 1000. Larger numbers are represented by combining these symbols in different ways.

Converting Arabic numerals to Roman numerals involves finding the largest Roman numeral that is less than or equal to the given number, subtracting it from the given number, and repeating the process until the number becomes zero. The Roman numerals obtained from each subtraction are concatenated to obtain the final Roman numeral equivalent.

Converting Roman numerals to Arabic numerals involves adding up the numerical values of the symbols, taking into account special cases where a smaller symbol is placed before a larger symbol (such as IV for 4) or where a symbol is repeated up to three times in a row (such as III for 3).
